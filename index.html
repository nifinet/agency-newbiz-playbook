Part 01 // The Problem

Why agencies suck at new business

Here's the uncomfortable truth about running an agency. You're great at the work. You're terrible at filling the pipeline.

Not because you're lazy. Because the way agencies find new business is fundamentally broken.

The Referral Trap

Most agencies get 70-80% of their revenue from referrals and word-of-mouth. That sounds nice until you look at the math: you have zero control over when the next referral comes in. One quarter you're turning work away. The next quarter you're wondering how to make payroll.

Referral dependency = revenue rollercoaster.

The Founder Bottleneck

In most agencies under 50 people, the founder IS the biz dev team. You're the one taking the calls, writing the proposals, doing the pitches. But you're also running the business, managing clients, and putting out fires every day.

New business only happens in the gaps between client work. Which means it doesn't happen consistently.

Cold Outreach Doesn't Work for Services

You've tried it. Or you've thought about it. Cold emails selling a $50K-$100K engagement? Nobody buys agency services from a cold email. The average cold reply rate is 3%. For high-ticket services, it's closer to 1%.

The problem isn't your copy. It's timing. You're reaching people who have no reason to care about you right now.

But here's what most agency owners miss:

You don't need hundreds of leads. You need 3-5 qualified conversations per week. People who actually have a project, actually have budget, and actually want to talk to an agency. That's enough to fill your pipeline and smooth out the rollercoaster.

The fix isn't more outreach. It's better timing.
What if you only reached out to people who just showed a buying signal? A new CMO who needs agencies. A founder who just posted about rebranding. A company that just raised and will invest in marketing.

Part 04 // Messaging

How to message prospects (without sounding like every other agency)

Agency outreach is different from SaaS outreach. You're not selling a $99/month subscription. You're starting a relationship that could be worth $50K-$200K over a year. The tone matters. The approach matters. Here are the rules.

The rules for agency outreach

  1. 01 Founder-to-founder. Write like a peer, not a salesperson. "I run an agency too" beats "Our agency specializes in."
  2. 02 Reference the signal. Always mention what they did. That's what makes this work.
  3. 03 No pitch deck in the first email. Never. The goal is a 15-minute call, not a proposal.
  4. 04 One proof point. One client name, one result, one number. Not a capabilities list.
  5. 05 No "we're the best agency." No "innovative solutions." No "holistic approach." Write like a human.
  6. 06 4-6 sentences max. You're busy. They're busy. Get to the point.

Example 1: New CMO Signal

Sarah just became VP Marketing at a 200-person SaaS company 2 weeks ago. Your agency does B2B SaaS branding.

Subject: Congrats on the VP Marketing role at DataCo

Sarah,

Saw you just joined DataCo. Congrats. The first few months in a new role are when most marketing leaders rethink their agency relationships.

We work with B2B SaaS companies your size on brand and positioning. Did a full rebrand for Qonto last year that contributed to a 40% increase in inbound leads within 6 months.

If you're re-evaluating anything on the brand side, happy to share what we've seen work. 15 min this week?

Example 2: Project Intent Signal

Lisa posted on LinkedIn: "Looking for a performance agency. Any recommendations?" Your agency does paid media.

Subject: Re: your post about performance agencies

Lisa,

Saw your post about looking for a performance agency. We've been doing paid media for growth-stage companies for 7 years. Recently helped Rebase (similar stage to you) cut their CAC by 35% in Q1 while scaling spend 2x.

No pitch. Just happy to share what we've seen work at your stage. Open to a quick call?

Example 3: Competitor Engagement Signal

Marc liked a post from a competing branding agency about "how to choose a branding partner." He's CMO at a 450-person company.

Subject: Choosing a branding partner

Marc,

Noticed you've been looking into the branding agency space. If you're actively evaluating, one thing we've found matters most: whether the agency has done this specifically in your industry.

We've worked with 12 companies in the enterprise SaaS space on brand strategy. Scaleway's recent positioning work was ours.

Worth 15 minutes to compare notes?
